The site is quiet, typographic, and content-first — the animation should be too. Each study below is a working demo of one idea, built the way it would ship: transforms and opacity only, springs where physics helps, everything honouring reduced-motion. The kind of polish you feel before you notice it.

rule 01

Motion explains — arrival, hierarchy, response. Never decoration for its own sake.

rule 02

Animate only transform and opacity. The compositor does the work; the page never janks.

rule 03

One orchestrated moment per page. Everything else stays under 300 ms.

rule 04

prefers-reduced-motion is a contract, not a suggestion. Every demo here honours it.

Your system asks for reduced motion, so the demos are holding still — which is itself the point of rule 04. Turn the OS setting off to see them move.

01

arrival — the staggered reveal

where it lives: the h1 + intro,
once per visit

The headline you read above didn't fade in as a block — each word rose a little, a few milliseconds after its neighbour. Stagger is the whole trick: it turns "content appeared" into "content was set in place." Tune it below; watch how fast it tips from elegant into showy.

hello, i'm julien. i make museums think.

software engineer 💻  ·  creative technologist 💡  ·  since 2016

why it works

Reading order is left-to-right, top-to-bottom; the stagger animates in reading order, so the motion rehearses what your eye was about to do anyway. Around 20–35 ms per word it reads as craftsmanship; past 60 ms it starts performing, and the linear easing shows how much the deceleration curve carries. The "spring" option is a real damped spring sampled into a CSS linear() timing function — physics, exported to the compositor.

shipping it

  • Web Animations API, zero dependencies
  • runs once per page load, ~700 ms total
  • reduced motion: content simply appears
  • anime.js buys the same in one line, if timelines grow

why it works

The reveal confirms "you've arrived at something new" and then gets out of the way. Untick animate once only and scroll up and down a few times — elements that re-animate on every pass turn the page into a jack-in-the-box. The discipline (small distance, one direction, one time) is what separates this from every over-eager marketing page.

shipping it

  • observer disconnects after firing — zero idle cost
  • reduced motion: entries are simply visible
03

pointing — springs, not easings

where it lives: hover states,
the ° on every link

Nature of Code, chapter three: a damped spring. Easings replay a recorded curve; springs respond to where things actually are, so interruptions feel continuous instead of restarting. Move your cursor through the field — the dot is solving F = −kx − cv every frame.

ζ = 0.52 · underdamped

applied: the degree mark pops on hover education° work° projects°

why it works

The readout above the graph is the damping ratio ζ = c / 2√(km). Below 1 the system overshoots (personality); at exactly 1 it settles as fast as physics allows (professionalism); above 1 it oozes (bureaucracy). UI wants ζ around 0.8–1: the merest hint of overshoot. That's the number behind why Apple's sheets feel right and why "wobbly" here feels like a toy.

shipping it

  • one 20-line spring class, or CSS linear() for fire-and-forget pops
  • only interactive elements get springs — never ambient ones
  • reduced motion: states snap instantly
04

ambience — the ascii field

where it lives: hero background,
or a 404 page with a pulse

The Kovalchuk reference, rebuilt honestly: a luminance field (three drifting metaballs) sampled on a grid and printed as type. The cursor is not an attractor — the field ignores it until you drift close to a blob, and only then does it energise. Atmosphere made of the site's own material — characters — with no video, no WebGL, no library.

why it works

Ambient motion is the riskiest idea on this page — it runs forever, so it must be cheap and calm. This one drifts at walking pace, ignores the cursor until you approach a blob (ambient systems react to approach; they don't chase), and pauses itself the moment it scrolls offscreen (watch the fps counter stop). Try the bear alphabet: the field renders in ʕ • ᴥ °, which is about as on-brand as physics gets.

shipping it

  • one canvas, one rAF loop, ~80 lines
  • IntersectionObserver gates the loop — zero cost offscreen
  • reduced motion: renders a single still frame
  • keep contrast low if type sits above it
05

character — the living bear

where it lives: the header,
on every page, forever

The logo is already a face. Faces blink. This bear watches your cursor (eyes on springs from study 03), blinks on its own schedule, and squints happily when clicked — the one in the page header up top is alive too. It's four glyphs and a spring; it's also the thing visitors will tell someone else about.

ʕ•ᴥ•ʔ

it's watching. click it.

why it works

This is the page's one permitted indulgence — and it obeys the same physics as everything else. The eye travel is capped at a few pixels so it never distorts the mark, blinks are 120 ms (real blinks are 100–150), and the idle cost is one cheap transform. Micro-character beats macro-spectacle: nobody remembers a parallax hero, everybody remembers that the bear looked at them.

shipping it

  • five <span>s in the header partial, one small script
  • degrades to the plain ʕ•ᴥ•ʔ with JS off
  • reduced motion: it still blinks — eyes don't track
06

restraint — the mirror test

where it lives: in every
code review, hopefully

Chanel's rule: before leaving the house, remove one accessory. Both cards below animate. Hover each. One of them you'd ship; the other is what happens when every idea survives.

why it works

Nothing on the right is technically wrong — every effect exists on award-winning sites, just not all at once, and not uninvited. The left card spends its entire motion budget on answering one question: am I interactive? Two milliseconds of your attention, then silence. Restraint isn't the absence of animation; it's animation with a job description.

shipping it

  • budget: one ambient element per page, max (study 04 or 05 — not both at full volume)
  • hovers ≤ 150 ms, reveals ≤ 600 ms
  • if unsure whether an effect earns its place, it doesn't

hugo-friendly, budget-friendly
